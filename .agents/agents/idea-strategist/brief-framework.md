# Creative Brief Framework

This framework is used by the Idea Strategist to turn any raw idea into a production-ready creative brief.

---

## 1. Input Diagnosis

Analyze the raw idea through these questions:

- What is the subject?
- What is the transformation?
- What is the emotional center?
- What is the visual promise?
- What is the audience supposed to remember?

---

## 2. Core Concept

The core concept must answer:

- Who or what is this about?
- What happens?
- Why does it matter?
- What makes it visually or emotionally interesting?

Use this format:

```text
A [format] about [subject] who/that [action or conflict], creating a feeling of [emotion] through [visual/narrative device].
```

Example:

```text
A 30-second cinematic teaser about a lonely DJ walking through futuristic Bogotá, where every step activates hidden rhythms in the city, creating a feeling of mystery, power, and anticipation.
```

---

## 3. Strategic Angle

The strategic angle is the creative thesis.

It should define what makes the idea different.

Examples:

- Not a DJ performance video — a city awakening through sound.
- Not a product ad — a ritual of transformation.
- Not a fashion film — a character study through movement and texture.

---

## 4. Audience

Define the audience by desire, not demographics only.

Weak:

```text
Young people ages 18-35.
```

Strong:

```text
People who are attracted to underground music culture, nocturnal aesthetics, futuristic cities, and cinematic visual identity.
```

---

## 5. Emotional Promise

Choose one primary emotion and two secondary emotions.

Examples:

- Primary: anticipation
  Secondary: mystery, elegance
- Primary: wonder
  Secondary: nostalgia, curiosity
- Primary: tension
  Secondary: obsession, danger

---

## 6. Narrative Direction

Use one of these structures:

### Reveal Structure

```text
Mystery → Discovery → Transformation → Final Iconic Image
```

### Ritual Structure

```text
Preparation → Movement → Activation → Release
```

### Contrast Structure

```text
Ordinary World → Strange Interruption → Escalation → New Reality
```

### Descent Structure

```text
Surface → Deeper Layer → Hidden Truth → Consequence
```

### Product Myth Structure

```text
Object → Desire → Ritual → Proof → Symbol
```

---

## 7. Visual Direction

Define:

- World.
- Color palette.
- Lighting.
- Texture.
- Camera style.
- Pacing.
- References.
- What to avoid.

Example:

```text
World: futuristic Bogotá at night
Palette: black, wet asphalt, electric blue, sodium orange
Lighting: neon reflections, practical street lights, soft fog
Texture: rain, concrete, glass, metallic surfaces
Camera: slow tracking, low angles, 35mm cinematic lens
Pacing: slow tension building into rhythmic acceleration
Avoid: cyberpunk clichés, random holograms, generic neon overload
```

---

## 8. Platform Fit

Decide:

- Best platform.
- Recommended duration.
- Aspect ratio.
- Pacing.
- Opening strategy.

Examples:

- TikTok/Reels — 20 to 35 seconds — vertical 9:16 — immediate visual hook in first second.
- YouTube — 2 to 4 minutes — 16:9 — slower narrative build and stronger worldbuilding.

---

## 9. Creative Risks

Always identify possible failure points:

- Too generic.
- Too abstract.
- Too slow.
- Too much exposition.
- Inconsistent visual world.
- Overloaded effects.
- Weak ending.
- No emotional payoff.

---

## 10. Next Agent Recommendation

Recommend only one or two next agents.

Examples:

- Next: `scriptwriter`
  Reason: the concept is clear and needs narrative structure.
- Next: `character-designer` + `scriptwriter`
  Reason: the character identity is central to the story.
- Next: `image-director`
  Reason: this is a visual concept prompt, not a narrative video.
